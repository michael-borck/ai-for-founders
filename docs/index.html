Three strategies that keep you in charge

// it is the strokes, not the racquet

Give a professional tennis player any racquet and they beat you. Give you the best racquet in the world and they still beat you. The tool barely matters; how you play does. These three ways of working keep the thinking, and the judgment, with you.

Strategy 1

Devil’s advocate

Tell the AI to argue against you. It is far more useful as a critic than a cheerleader, so point it at your own idea and let it attack.

It attacks, you decide what survives. You stay the judge, not the jury.
Show example prompt 
Be a skeptical seed investor who has seen 500 pitches. Attack this idea: find the three weakest assumptions and the one that would kill it. Then list the questions I must answer before I build. Here it is: [your idea].
Strategy 2

Board of directors

Put the same question to a room, not a single voice. Ask for several people at once, each with their own job and their own bias, then weigh them yourself.

You get perspectives, not an answer. The synthesis is yours.
Show example prompt 
Answer as a panel, each in their own voice: (1) a cautious CFO, (2) my most skeptical customer, (3) a growth-hungry marketer. Each gives an honest take on this decision, then stops. I will decide. The decision: [your decision].
Strategy 3 · the important one

Two windows: converse, then produce

Keep two chats open. One is for thinking: argue, explore, make a mess. The other is your actual work. The AI never writes into the second one. You hand-carry across only the lines worth keeping.

Window 1 · converse

Think out loud. Challenge it. Try things. This one is allowed to be messy.

→you choose
Window 2 · produce

Your real document. Only the lines you decided are good make it across.

The copy-and-paste is the judgment. You are the human in the loop, choosing what crosses over. Conversation, not Delegation, made physical.

The trust tool: when to lean in, when to stay in charge

// two questions, four corners

AI gives you a convincing average — fluent, usually about right, but “about right” and “exactly right” are not the same thing. Two questions sort any task into one of four corners: do you need an average or precision, and are the stakes small or large?

Small reversible, cheap to get wrong
Large money, reputation, the relationship
Average is fine ballpark is enough
Lean in.

Brainstorm names, draft a post, generate customer-discovery questions, list competitor angles, rewrite for clarity. This is its home turf.

Lean in, with a glance.

Summarise 20 customer calls for themes — but skim the raw notes before you act, or it smooths away the one that matters. The pitch story, not its numbers.

Precision required close is the same as wrong
Use, then verify.

A code snippet (run it). A cited stat for a casual deck — check the source before it leaves your laptop.

Human owns it.

Every number in your financial model. Legal and compliance claims. The go/no-go. Pricing. What you promise a customer.

Rule of thumb: where it hurts to be wrong — money, reputation, the relationship — you stay in charge.

Run it private, on your own laptop

// no subscription, no data leaving the machine

The tool is the racquet, not the strokes, so any of these will do. You can run capable AI locally for free. It is weaker than a frontier model at the hardest tasks, but more than enough for the strategies above, and it never sends your work to anyone.

start here

Msty

One installer, no setup. Picks a model for you, chats with your own documents, runs offline. The smoothest first step for a beginner.

GPT4All

Why bother going local? Your unvalidated idea is often your only asset. Thinking it through with a local model means you are not posting it into someone else’s cloud. On a modest laptop, start with a small model (3 to 4 billion parameters) and work up.

Go deeper: self-paced exercises

// do these in your own time

Four hands-on tasks from the workshop, each a self-contained hour or less. Pick one, use whatever AI tool you have, and produce something real.

~40 min

AI tool test drive

Give the same real task to two or three AI tools and compare quality, tone, and gaps. Learn which tool suits which job.

Framework: AI Task Suitability Spectrum · Output: your notes on tool strengths
~60 min

Prompting in three rounds

Take a workplace scenario through three rounds: a naive prompt, then the RTCF pattern, then advanced moves like chain-of-thought and iteration. Feel the jump.

Framework: RTCF · Output: a personal prompt library
~45 min

Data analysis challenge

Hand a real dataset to your AI, answer three business questions, then verify its findings yourself. Trust, but check.

Framework: AI-Assisted Analysis Workflow · Output: a one-page insight brief
~40 min

Redesign your workflow

Map a task you do every week, classify each step with the Opportunity Finder, and rebuild it with AI where it actually adds value. Test one step live.

Framework: AI Opportunity Finder · Output: a before/after workflow
